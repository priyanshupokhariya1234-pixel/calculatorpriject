Logarithms

Definition

A logarithm is the inverse operation of exponentiation. It answers the question: "To what power must the base be raised to get a certain number?"

If aˣ = b, then logₐ(b) = x
Read as: "log base a of b equals x"

Understanding Logarithms

Logarithms are the "opposite" of exponents:

  • If 2³ = 8, then log₂(8) = 3
  • If 10² = 100, then log₁₀(100) = 2
  • If e¹ = e, then ln(e) = 1 (natural log)

Common Types

Common Logarithm (Base 10)

log₁₀(x) or simply log(x)

Used in scientific notation and many calculations.

Natural Logarithm (Base e)

logₑ(x) or ln(x)

Where e ≈ 2.71828. Used in calculus, physics, and growth models.

Binary Logarithm (Base 2)

log₂(x)

Used in computer science and information theory.

Key Properties

Product Rule

log(xy) = log(x) + log(y)

Logarithm of a product equals sum of logarithms.

Quotient Rule

log(x/y) = log(x) - log(y)

Logarithm of a quotient equals difference of logarithms.

Power Rule

log(xⁿ) = n · log(x)

Logarithm of a power equals exponent times logarithm.

Change of Base Formula

logₐ(b) = log(b) / log(a) = ln(b) / ln(a)

Convert between different bases.

Special Values

  • log(1) = 0 (any base)
  • logₐ(a) = 1
  • logₐ(aⁿ) = n

Applications

  • Scientific Notation: Simplifying very large or small numbers
  • Growth Models: Population growth, compound interest, radioactive decay
  • Signal Processing: Decibels (dB) use logarithmic scales
  • Computer Science: Algorithm complexity, binary search
  • Chemistry: pH scale (pH = -log[H⁺])

Solving Logarithmic Equations

Example 1: Solve log₂(x) = 5

  • Convert to exponential: x = 2⁵ = 32

Example 2: Solve log(x) + log(3) = log(12)

  • Use product rule: log(3x) = log(12)
  • Since logs are equal: 3x = 12
  • Solution: x = 4

Graph Characteristics

  • Logarithmic functions are the inverse of exponential functions
  • Domain: (0, ∞) - only positive numbers
  • Range: (-∞, ∞) - all real numbers
  • Always passes through (1, 0)
  • Vertical asymptote at x = 0
  • Increases slowly (much slower than exponential growth)

Real-World Example: pH Scale

The pH of a solution measures acidity:

pH = -log₁₀[H⁺]

Where [H⁺] is the hydrogen ion concentration.

  • pH = 7: neutral (pure water)
  • pH < 7: acidic
  • pH > 7: basic

Key Tips

  • Remember: logarithms are only defined for positive numbers
  • Use properties to simplify complex logarithmic expressions
  • When solving equations, always check your answers (logs require positive arguments)
  • Natural logs (ln) are especially useful in calculus
  • Logarithmic scales make it easier to work with very large ranges of values
